Street Medicine

I believe strongly in helping people avoid this crisis, but also in helping them get out of it. This is a challenging problem because of the nature of modern drugs, which are often used for self-medication by desperate individuals. These drugs, particularly opioids and methamphetamine, create usage disorders that push individuals to use even when their lives are being destroyed. This makes care very difficult, as relapse and self-destruction are just part of the disease.

Nevertheless, without support, these individuals end up bouncing in and out of jail and the emergency department until a severe illness or accident finishes them off.

This is a solvable problem that we as a society have created by non-sensical drug and housing policy. While my work will not solve the core issues that lead to this problem, it does reduce the risk of death and serious disease amongst those who I serve.

What Do I Do?

I travel to homeless encampments of any size throughout the bay area and into the central valley, primarily in San Mateo and the south bay.

  • Naloxone/fentanyl test strip distribution and use education
  • Assistance with access to resources
  • Wound care and encouragement to access health care when needed

How Can You Help?

A donation of any size can help. There are multiple ways to donate, just head over to the donation page. Cash donations are used to purchase:

  • Fentanyl test strips
  • Wound care supplies
  • Clothing items
  • Sometimes food and water

Root of the Problem

America's struggle with homelessness and potentially lethal drug dependence is long and complex. Since the 1970's people with complex mental health issues have been "de-institutionalized"— initially billed as a kindness to prevent forced confinement, but without any real funding for caring for those who struggle to care for themselves but are not a direct danger to themselves or others. The result is that now a substantial burden of mental health struggle takes place on the streets with minimal support. Homeless shelters, while valuable for many, are notoriously dangerous to the point that many prefer the streets.

Added to this are the ups and downs of the economy in a country with confusing social supports (many live on disability, but some who really need it struggle to access it). In the San Francisco Bay Area, where I live, the cost of housing has become completely unaffordable, resulting in large increases in homelessness whenever there is an economic downturn. During the COVID-19 pandemic, the rate of homelessness in this area increased by nearly 40%.

This situation is made more complex by the rise of very strong recreational drugs. In the 1990s and 2000s, the Sackler family directed Purdue Pharma to fraudulently market OxyContin as a 'non-addictive' pain killer, which is a lie. They then pushed the drug on physicians and consumers with extremely aggressive marketing practices. This directly resulted in a massive increase in addiction to opioids in our country, partially fueled by the loss of jobs to other countries and the increasing cost of living and fragmentation of society. Drug traffickers took advantage of this state of affairs and produced first highly potent heroin and then a variety of super-powerful fentanyl analogs. As doctors realized they had been lied to, they stopped prescribing prescription pain pills, and addicts were shunted to these dangerous alternatives. Unfortunately, at the same time the Mexican government clamped down on ephedrine importing resulting in the development of a new method of synthesizing meth that uses harsh chemical instead. This new method resulted in a very different drug, rather than being a euphoric party drug, it instead resulted in a dramatic upsurge in psychosis, serious mental health problems, and heart failure.

As a result of the above, huge numbers of people died and many more ended up on the street with new severe addiction, mental health crisis, and health issues. Those who were already homeless, particularly those with existing mental health struggles, suddenly had extremely dangerous cheap drugs available, which killed them in record numbers, triggered psychosis, and made it even harder for them to access services.

Now we live in a world with booming homelessness and overlapping drug addition. The SF bay area has more services available for these communities than the vast majority of this country, but it is still not enough. This is particularly true now as the most vulnerable—those struggling with homelessness, addiction, and mental health issues—are much less likely to travel to access services. Many remain on the street and self-medicate with a mix of substances. These people have a very high burden of health issues including chronic wounds and chronic medical conditions, but little access to care in part because of their addictions. Although, even those with no substance addictions can still struggle.
